From the editor

On smoke,
spice, & the
long simmer.

A short note on why this volume exists, and on the kitchens it grew out of.

Indonesia does not cook with one voice. Sumatran kitchens reduce coconut milk for hours until it turns oil-black; Javanese ones lean on palm sugar, soy and the slow patience of gulai; Sundanese cooks worship freshness — raw greens, a pestle of sambal, rice still steaming. Bali grinds spice paste with lemongrass and lime leaf and wraps it around fish. Bandung deep-fries everything.

What unites these tables is not technique, nor even a shared ingredient — it is a relationship to heat. The chilli, the charcoal, the deep wok, the patient stockpot. Smoke that hangs in the warung's bamboo rafters. The quick fry of a tempeh, half-cooked on purpose. The long, dark wedding of beef and coconut that eventually becomes rendang.

The fifteen recipes that follow are not the whole story; they are an opening. Cook one a week and you will have eaten Indonesia for almost four months. Cook them in order and you will have crossed Sumatra, Java, Bali and back. Cook them out of order — which is, of course, how anyone actually cooks — and you will simply have eaten very well.

Java  /  Indonesia

Nasi Goreng

Fried rice, eaten at any hour — especially the small ones.

Serves
2
Time
25 min
Difficulty
Easy
Region
Java-wide

No dish travels through the night quite like nasi goreng. It is what the warung makes when the rice is yesterday's, when the wok is already hot, when the last of the sweet soy is in the bottle. It is what a Jakarta street-cook ladles onto a plate at one in the morning while a moped engine cools beside the cart. The trick is not in the ingredients — every household has a different version — but in the heat: high, fast, never crowded.

Use cold rice. Day-old rice from the fridge is non-negotiable; freshly steamed grains will steam, not fry. Pound your spice paste, do not blend it — a mortar bruises shallot and garlic differently, and the wok will know.

Kecap manis is to nasi goreng what soy sauce is to fried rice everywhere else — except that it is also sugar, and molasses, and a small religion. — A warung cook, Yogyakarta

Finish with a fried egg whose yolk is still loose, a snow of fried shallots, sliced cucumber, tomato wedges, and a few sheets of kerupuk shattered over the top. Sambal on the side — never on top. Eat with a spoon.

Ingredients — bahan

  • 400 gday-old jasmine rice, broken up with wet hands
  • 4shallots, peeled
  • 3cloves garlic
  • 2–3red bird's-eye chillies, to taste
  • 1 tspshrimp paste (terasi), toasted
  • 150 gchicken thigh or peeled prawns, diced
  • 2eggs, plus 2 for frying on top
  • 3 Tbspkecap manis (sweet soy)
  • 1 Tbsplight soy sauce
  • 2 stalksscallion, sliced
  • to servefried shallots, cucumber, tomato, prawn crackers, sambal

Method — cara

  1. Pound shallots, garlic, chillies and toasted shrimp paste in a mortar to a coarse paste.
  2. Heat 2 Tbsp neutral oil in a wok over the highest flame you have. When it shimmers, add the paste. Fry until it turns one shade darker and smells sweet, 90 seconds.
  3. Add the diced chicken or prawn. Toss until just cooked, 2 minutes.
  4. Push everything to the side. Crack 2 eggs into the cleared space. Scramble loosely, then break up the curd.
  5. Add the cold rice. Toss in long arcs — do not stir. Let it sit against the metal between tosses so the grains catch.
  6. Stream the kecap manis around the wok rim, then the light soy. Toss until every grain wears a copper coat, 2 minutes.
  7. Off the heat, fold in scallions. Plate. Top each portion with a fried egg whose edges are lace.
  8. Garnish with fried shallots, cucumber half-moons, tomato, and shattered prawn crackers. Sambal on the side.
Chef's note

If your kecap manis is thin, simmer it with a thumb of palm sugar for two minutes and let it cool — it will glaze the rice like lacquer.

№002
West Sumatra  /  Padang

Nasi Padang

Not a dish, but a table — the Minang spread of many small bowls.

Serves
4–6
Time
3+ hours
Difficulty
Project
Region
Minangkabau

Walk into a rumah makan Padang anywhere from Bukittinggi to Rotterdam and the same theatre unfolds: a waiter approaches the table balancing twelve, fifteen, twenty small plates up his arms like a pianist's chord. He sets them all down. You eat what you touch; the rest is taken away and you pay only for what you ate. The food does not arrive — it is unfurled.

At its heart is a mound of steamed rice. Around it: the obligatory rendang (see №014), a green chilli sambal hijau the colour of new tea leaves, a brothy gulai of leafy greens, a piece of fried fish, perhaps ayam pop — pale poached chicken with a brick-red sambal, and a ladle of curry sauce poured greedily over the rice. The Minang refer to this last act as kuah disiram, "the sauce is bathed on."

At a Padang table you do not order food. You order rice. Everything else is the negotiation.

Below is the abbreviated version: a single Padang plate to assemble at home. Make the rendang the day before. Today you will only need to fry, blend, and arrange.

Ingredients — the spread

  • 800 gjasmine rice, steamed
  • 1 bowlbeef rendang (recipe №014)
  • 4chicken thighs, for ayam pop
  • 1 bunchcassava leaves or kale, for gulai daun
  • 400 mlcoconut milk
  • 12green bird's-eye chillies
  • 6green long chillies
  • 3shallots, 2 garlic, 1 tomato
  • 2kaffir lime leaves, 2 salam leaves
  • 1 thumbgalangal, bruised; 1 stalk lemongrass
  • to servefried boiled egg, sliced cucumber, kerupuk

Method — the table

  1. For ayam pop: poach chicken thighs in coconut water with garlic and salt for 25 minutes. Drain, then pan-fry briefly in oil until just blushing — they should remain pale.
  2. For sambal hijau: blanch the green chillies and shallots for 30 seconds, then pound coarsely with garlic, tomato and salt. Fry in 3 Tbsp oil with a kaffir lime leaf until darkened, 4 minutes.
  3. For gulai daun: simmer coconut milk with bruised galangal, lemongrass, salam leaves and a pinch of turmeric for 10 minutes. Add the greens; cook 5 minutes more.
  4. Reheat the rendang gently.
  5. To plate: a generous heap of rice in the centre. Around it, a piece of ayam pop, a spoon of sambal hijau, a small bowl of gulai, a piece of rendang, a halved fried egg, cucumber, kerupuk.
  6. Ladle a spoon of gulai sauce over a corner of the rice. Eat with your right hand or a spoon and fork — the fork pushes, the spoon scoops.
Chef's note

A Padang plate is judged by its sambal first. If your green sambal does not bite you back, add another chilli; do not add salt.

Central Java  /  Banyumas

Soto Purbalingga

Also called sroto Sokaraja — the only soto with a peanut sauce.

Serves
4
Time
1 h 30 min
Difficulty
Medium
Region
Banyumas

Every region of Java has a soto, and every soto is a slight argument with the others. Purbalingga's version — sometimes labelled sroto Sokaraja after the neighbouring town — does what no other soto does: it ends with a spoonful of peanut-and-chilli sauce stirred into the bowl at the last second. The broth turns rust-orange. The whole soup gains weight.

It is a beef-and-chicken broth, golden with turmeric, served over pressed rice cakes (ketupat) with beansprouts, fried shallots, scallion, and rice noodles. The peanut sauce is similar to a gado-gado dressing but thinner, brighter, less sweet. A traditional bowl also receives a few squares of crisp kerupuk on top, which collapse luxuriously into the soup.

A bowl of sroto travels well; the peanut sauce is added at the table, never in the pot.

Ingredients — bahan

  • 500 gbeef brisket, in one piece
  • 2chicken legs (for double-stock)
  • 2 Lcold water
  • 6shallots, 4 cloves garlic
  • 2 thumbsginger, 1 thumb turmeric
  • 3candlenuts (or macadamia)
  • 2 stalkslemongrass, bruised
  • 4kaffir lime leaves, 2 salam leaves
  • for sauce100 g roasted peanuts, 4 chillies, 1 garlic, 1 Tbsp palm sugar, splash tamarind
  • to serveketupat or rice, vermicelli, beansprouts, scallion, fried shallots, kerupuk merah

Method — cara

  1. Cover beef and chicken with cold water, bring to a gentle simmer, skimming foam. Cook 45 minutes uncovered.
  2. Meanwhile, pound shallots, garlic, ginger, turmeric and candlenuts to a paste. Fry in 3 Tbsp oil until fragrant and oil splits, 4 minutes.
  3. Add the paste to the simmering broth with bruised lemongrass, kaffir and salam leaves. Continue cooking 30 minutes more, until beef is fork-tender.
  4. Lift out meats, slice thin, return scraps to the pot. Season the broth with salt and a teaspoon of sugar.
  5. For the peanut sauce: pound peanuts with fried chillies, garlic, palm sugar, tamarind and a pinch of salt to a thick paste. Thin with 6 Tbsp hot broth.
  6. In each bowl: a few ketupat cubes or rice, a nest of softened vermicelli, beansprouts, sliced meat. Ladle hot broth over.
  7. Finish with a spoon of peanut sauce, scallion, fried shallots, and a couple of kerupuk on top.
Chef's note

For a Sokaraja-style bowl, look for kerupuk merah — bright pink prawn crackers. They are decorative and structural; do not skip them.

№004
Central Java  /  Banyumas

Mendoan

Half-fried tempeh, soft in the middle, eaten while still snapping.

Serves
2–3
Time
20 min
Difficulty
Easy
Region
Banyumas

Mendoan is the most counter-intuitive thing in Indonesian frying: tempeh battered, dropped briefly into hot oil, and pulled out before it is fully cooked. The word itself, in Banyumas Javanese, means "half-done." The skin is barely set; the inside is still tender, almost custardy from the nutty soy paste of the tempeh. You eat it at a roadside stall in Purwokerto, dipped in soy sauce with a sliced bird's-eye chilli floating in it, between two cups of tea.

The batter is rice flour with turmeric and ground coriander, fragrant with garlic and minced scallion. The tempeh must be very thin — proper mendoan tempeh is sold in wide, paper-thin sheets specifically for this dish.

Mendoan is not crispy. If it is crispy, you have made tempe goreng. They are different foods.

Ingredients — bahan

  • 300 gtempeh, sliced as thin as possible (3 mm)
  • 120 grice flour
  • 2 Tbspplain flour
  • 1 tspground coriander
  • ½ tspground turmeric
  • 3cloves garlic, finely grated
  • 2 stalksscallion, very finely sliced
  • 1 tspsalt, a pinch of sugar
  • ~200 mlcold water
  • for servingkecap manis with sliced bird's-eye chillies

Method — cara

  1. Whisk the dry ingredients in a wide bowl. Add garlic, scallion, and gradually whisk in cold water to a pancake-batter consistency.
  2. Heat 1.5 cm of neutral oil in a wide pan to about 180 °C — hot but not smoking.
  3. Dip each tempeh slice in the batter, letting excess drip off briefly.
  4. Slide into the oil. Fry 60–90 seconds total, flipping once. The batter should just set — pale yellow, not brown.
  5. Drain very briefly on paper. Eat immediately, dipped into kecap manis with a slice of fresh bird's-eye chilli.
Chef's note

If your tempeh sheets aren't paper-thin, butterfly each piece — a 3 mm slice opens to a 1.5 mm sheet, which is exactly what mendoan wants.

North Sumatra  /  Medan

Sop Durian

The dessert soup of the king of fruits.

Serves
4
Time
15 min
Difficulty
Easy
Region
Sumatra

In Medan, at the night markets behind the cathedral, vendors keep two things on the counter: ripe durian, and shaved ice. From these and a few small additions emerges sop durian — a sweet soup that is half-dessert, half-shock. Flesh from a generously ripe durian is set in the bottom of a tall glass, drowned in chilled coconut milk sweetened with palm sugar and scented with pandan, then topped with shaved ice. Black glutinous rice or jellies are optional accomplices.

It is not a recipe in the technical sense — it is an assembly. The difficulty is entirely upstream: locating a durian that is properly ripe, neither stringy nor sour, and convincing it to give you its flesh whole.

Durian smells of everything and tastes of itself; here, ice and coconut play the chorus to its single, very loud aria.

Ingredients — bahan

  • 300 gripe durian flesh, seeds removed
  • 400 mlcoconut milk, cold
  • 80 gpalm sugar (gula merah), shaved
  • 2 leavespandan, knotted
  • 1 pinchsalt
  • to serveshaved ice, cooked black glutinous rice, sago pearls or grass jelly

Method — cara

  1. Combine palm sugar with 4 Tbsp water in a small pan with the pandan leaves. Warm gently until dissolved. Let cool. Discard pandan.
  2. Whisk the palm syrup, coconut milk and a pinch of salt together. Chill thoroughly.
  3. To assemble: place a spoon of black glutinous rice or sago in the bottom of a tall glass. Add a generous handful of durian flesh.
  4. Pour the cold coconut over until almost full. Crown with shaved ice.
  5. Serve with a long spoon and a short attention span.
Chef's note

If durian is not at the market, this recipe works almost as well with very ripe mango or jackfruit — the texture is different but the assembly is right.

Sate Ayam

Charcoal chicken skewers with a peanut sauce that has weight.

Serves
4
Time
45 min + marinade
Difficulty
Medium
Region
Madura

A Madurese satay seller arrives at his corner of a city — Surabaya, Malang, Jakarta — with a small bamboo fan, a long brazier, a tin of skewers, and a bottle of kecap manis. He fans, the coals glow, the bird begins to char. By the time the skewers are turned, the air around him is sweet, smoky, slightly stinging. He sells them in tens, on a banana leaf, beside a heap of pressed rice cakes (lontong) and a brown pool of peanut sauce.

Marinate the chicken not for tenderness — thigh handles fire on its own — but for flavour. Kecap manis caramelises onto the skewer; the meat takes a lacquer. The peanut sauce is its own affair: pounded roasted peanuts, kecap manis, garlic, fried shallot, a whisper of lime.

A Madurese satay seller does not call. He fans. The smell calls.

Ingredients — bahan

  • 600 gboneless chicken thigh, in 2 cm cubes
  • 3 Tbspkecap manis, plus more for grilling
  • 2 Tbsplight soy sauce
  • 3cloves garlic, grated
  • 1 Tbspneutral oil
  • 1 tspground coriander
  • 20bamboo skewers, soaked
  • for sauce180 g roasted peanuts, 4 fried shallots, 2 cloves fried garlic, 4 Tbsp kecap manis, 1 Tbsp tamarind water, 2 red chillies, salt
  • to servelontong or rice, sliced shallot, lime, pickled cucumber

Method — cara

  1. Whisk kecap manis, soy, garlic, oil and coriander. Marinate the chicken at least 1 hour, preferably overnight.
  2. For the peanut sauce: pound peanuts, fried shallots, fried garlic and chillies to a thick paste. Stir in kecap manis, tamarind, and just enough hot water to make it spoonable. Salt to taste.
  3. Thread 4–5 cubes per skewer, packed tightly together — they shield each other from drying out.
  4. Light a charcoal grill. When the coals are white-hot, fan with vigour as you grill the skewers, 2–3 minutes per side. Brush twice with extra kecap manis during cooking.
  5. Arrange skewers on a plate with sliced shallot, a wedge of lime, and rice or lontong. Spoon the peanut sauce generously over the top.
Chef's note

Charcoal is non-negotiable. A gas flame will cook chicken; only embers will give satay its proper, smoke-shadowed sweetness.

Java  /  Chinese-Indonesian

Bakso

Beef meatball soup. A bell rings; a cart appears.

Serves
4
Time
1 h 30 min
Difficulty
Medium
Region
Java-wide

A bakso seller pushes a wooden cart through residential streets at dusk, banging a porcelain bowl with a spoon — tok, tok, tok — and within half a block, six children are at his elbow asking for a portion. The bowl: a clear beef broth, a knot of yellow noodles, two or three pale meatballs the size of small clementines, a square of fried tofu also stuffed with meatball mix, a sliced wonton, beansprouts, scallion, fried shallots. A spoon of sambal, a drizzle of black soy. Eat fast, before the broth cools.

The meatballs are the work. They are pounded — not minced, pounded — until the meat becomes a paste, with tapioca starch and ice water folded in so they remain bouncy and pale. A cook in Solo will tell you, gravely, that the bounce of a bakso is its honesty.

A meatball that does not bounce when dropped is a meatball that has lied about its ingredients.

Ingredients — bahan

  • 500 glean beef (eye round), very cold, in chunks
  • 120 gtapioca starch
  • 100 mliced water
  • 4cloves garlic, grated
  • 2 tspsalt, ½ tsp white pepper, ½ tsp baking powder
  • for broth1 kg beef bones, 1 onion, 4 cloves garlic, 1 thumb ginger, 1 tsp coriander seed, salt
  • to serveegg noodles, vermicelli, beansprouts, fried wontons, scallion, fried shallots, sweet soy, sambal, celery leaf

Method — cara

  1. Roast the bones at 220 °C for 30 minutes. Transfer to a stockpot, cover with 3 L cold water, bring to a simmer with onion, garlic, ginger and coriander. Skim, then cook 90 minutes. Strain and salt to taste.
  2. For the meatballs: blitz the very cold beef in a food processor with garlic, salt, pepper and baking powder until completely smooth — almost a pâté.
  3. Add iced water gradually, then the tapioca, pulsing until the mixture is glossy and stretchy.
  4. Bring a wide pan of salted water to a bare simmer (not a boil). Shape meatballs by squeezing the paste between thumb and forefinger and scooping with a wet spoon.
  5. Drop each one into the water as it forms. They will sink, then float. Cook 4 minutes after floating. Set aside.
  6. To serve: divide noodles, vermicelli and beansprouts among bowls. Top with 3–4 meatballs each, fried wontons, scallion. Ladle hot broth over. Finish with kecap manis, sambal, fried shallots, celery leaf.
Chef's note

Every step that is cold should stay cold. Warm meat absorbs starch unevenly and the bakso will turn floury — the enemy of bounce.

№008
Java  /  Jakarta

Bubur Ayam

Rice porridge, eaten at first light from a passing cart.

Serves
4
Time
1 h
Difficulty
Easy
Region
Java-wide

Before Jakarta wakes up, the bubur sellers are already on their corners. They cook the porridge overnight on a low flame so that by 5 a.m. the rice has dissolved entirely — what they ladle out is a soft, glossy white. Onto it goes shredded poached chicken, sliced scallion, fried shallots, soybeans, kerupuk, a length of crisp cakwe (Chinese fried dough), a drizzle of soy sauce, a spoon of yellow chicken jus, a smudge of sambal.

The question of whether to stir the bubur or eat it in layers is, in Jakarta, a question with two factions. Diaduk (stirred) belongs to the practical. Tidak diaduk (not stirred) belongs to the romantic. There is no third position.

Bubur is the only Indonesian breakfast eaten standing up; everyone is in a hurry.

Ingredients — bahan

  • 200 gjasmine rice, rinsed
  • 2 Lchicken stock (plus more)
  • 2chicken thighs, on the bone
  • 1 thumbginger, smashed
  • 2 clovesgarlic, smashed
  • 1 stalklemongrass, bruised
  • 2pandan leaves, knotted
  • 1 tspsalt, white pepper
  • for yellow jus2 shallots, 1 garlic, ½ tsp turmeric, reserved poaching liquid
  • to servecakwe, fried soybeans (kedelai goreng), scallion, fried shallots, kerupuk, kecap manis, sambal

Method — cara

  1. Poach the chicken in 1 L stock with ginger, garlic and lemongrass for 25 minutes. Lift out, cool, shred. Reserve the poaching liquid.
  2. In a heavy pot, combine rice with the remaining stock and pandan. Bring to a simmer; cook on the lowest possible heat, stirring often, until the rice dissolves — about 50 minutes. Add more stock if too thick. Salt to taste.
  3. For the yellow jus: pound shallots, garlic and turmeric to a paste. Fry in 1 Tbsp oil, then loosen with 200 ml of the reserved poaching liquid. Simmer 5 minutes.
  4. To serve: ladle bubur into a wide bowl. Pile shredded chicken on one side, fried soybeans on another, scattered fried shallots, scallion. Drape a length of cakwe across the top.
  5. At the table: a spoon of yellow jus, a drizzle of kecap manis, a smudge of sambal, a few sheets of kerupuk crumbled in. Stir, or don't.
Chef's note

If you cannot find cakwe (youtiao in Chinese bakeries), a thick slice of grilled sourdough, oiled and crisp at the edges, will keep the textural promise.

№009
Bali

Sate Lilit

Spiced minced fish, wrapped around a stalk of lemongrass.

Serves
4
Time
45 min
Difficulty
Medium
Region
Bali

Where Madurese satay is meat threaded onto a skewer, Balinese sate lilit is the inverse: the satay becomes the skewer. Minced fish (sometimes chicken, sometimes pork on the Hindu island) is bound with a thick yellow spice paste (base genep) and grated coconut, then moulded around the woody bottom of a lemongrass stalk. Grilled over coconut husks, the lemongrass perfumes the meat from inside.

It is served at temple festivals on small bamboo plates with rice and a few sambals — sambal matah (raw shallot, lemongrass, chilli, lime, coconut oil) being the proper accompaniment.

A skewer of sate lilit is, structurally, an entire Balinese kitchen — paste, coconut, fish, lemongrass — collapsed onto a stick.

Ingredients — bahan

  • 500 gfirm white fish (mackerel, snapper), minced
  • 80 gfreshly grated coconut
  • 12lemongrass stalks, root ends trimmed
  • for paste6 shallots, 4 garlic, 5 red chillies, 2 candlenuts, 1 tsp turmeric, 1 thumb ginger, 1 thumb galangal, 1 tsp coriander seed, 1 tsp shrimp paste
  • 2kaffir lime leaves, very finely sliced
  • 1egg white
  • 1 Tbsppalm sugar, salt
  • for sambal matah5 shallots, 1 lemongrass (tender white part), 4 chillies, 2 kaffir lime leaves, 1 lime, 3 Tbsp coconut oil, salt

Method — cara

  1. Pound or blitz the paste ingredients to a smooth purée. Fry in 3 Tbsp coconut oil over medium-low heat until oil splits and paste darkens, 8 minutes. Cool completely.
  2. In a bowl, combine minced fish, cooled paste, grated coconut, sliced lime leaves, egg white, palm sugar and salt. Knead with your hand until tacky — about 2 minutes.
  3. Bash the woody bottom 3 cm of each lemongrass stalk with the back of a knife to open the fibres.
  4. Mould 2 Tbsp of mixture around the bashed end of each stalk, squeezing until it adheres firmly.
  5. Grill over hot coals (or under a fierce broiler), turning, until the fish is opaque and edges are charred — 4 to 5 minutes.
  6. For sambal matah: slice shallots, lemongrass, chillies and lime leaves as finely as your knife will allow. Toss with lime, hot coconut oil and salt. Spoon over the satay at the table.
Chef's note

If lemongrass stalks are not stout enough, use them in pairs, side by side; the moulded fish will hold them together as it cooks.

№010
East Java  /  Madiun, Kediri

Nasi Pecel

Rice, blanched greens, and a peanut sauce that does the work.

Serves
4
Time
30 min
Difficulty
Easy
Region
East Java

Pecel is the salad of East Java — though calling it salad short-changes the sauce. A platter of blanched water spinach, beansprouts, long beans, cabbage, perhaps the tendril of a young papaya leaf, is heaped beside steamed rice and a few crisp rempeyek — peanut-studded rice crackers. Over the lot goes a thick, tan-coloured peanut sauce, spiked with kaffir lime leaf and tamarind. It is breakfast in Madiun, lunch in Kediri, and a virtuous late dinner anywhere.

The trick is to not dress the vegetables in the kitchen. Serve everything separate; let each eater pour their own sauce, in whatever quantity the day requires.

A pecel is what an Italian crudité would be if it had grown up eating chilli.

Ingredients — bahan

  • a bunchwater spinach (kangkung) or spinach
  • 150 gbeansprouts
  • 100 glong beans, in 4 cm lengths
  • ¼small cabbage, shredded
  • for sauce200 g roasted peanuts, 4 red chillies, 3 garlic cloves, 4 kaffir lime leaves, 50 g palm sugar, 1 Tbsp tamarind paste, 1 tsp salt
  • to servesteamed rice, rempeyek crackers, fried tempeh, lime wedges

Method — cara

  1. Blanch each vegetable separately in salted boiling water for 30 seconds; lift out into iced water. This preserves their colour and individual crunch.
  2. Toast the chillies and garlic over a dry pan until blackened in spots. Cool.
  3. In a mortar (or food processor), pound peanuts to a coarse meal. Add chillies, garlic, kaffir lime leaves and palm sugar — pound to a thick paste.
  4. Loosen with hot water, a few tablespoons at a time, until the sauce is the consistency of pourable peanut butter. Stir in tamarind and salt.
  5. To plate: a mound of rice; the vegetables arranged like petals around it; rempeyek and tempeh on the side. Dress with peanut sauce at the table.
Chef's note

The sauce will keep for two weeks in the fridge and improves overnight. Always reheat with a splash of hot water — never reheat dry.

№011
Jakarta

Ketoprak

A cart, a knife, a rolling stone, peanut sauce.

Serves
2
Time
30 min
Difficulty
Easy
Region
Jakarta

A ketoprak seller works on the lid of his cart as if it were a kitchen counter. Onto a banana-leaf-lined plate go cubes of pressed rice (lontong), rice vermicelli, blanched beansprouts and slices of pan-fried tofu. He pounds peanuts, garlic and chilli in a wooden mortar held in his lap, slacks the sauce with kecap manis and a splash of tamarind, then pours it over the plate. He shatters a few prawn crackers on top with the heel of his palm. The whole transaction takes ninety seconds.

Where gado-gado uses many vegetables, ketoprak is austere — its identity is rice noodles + tofu + beansprouts + a punchy peanut-garlic sauce. The word "ketoprak" itself, locals will tell you, is onomatopoeic: the rhythm of the seller's knife and pestle as he assembles.

The cart announces itself. Ke—to—prak.

Ingredients — bahan

  • 200 grice vermicelli, soaked until soft
  • 300 glontong or rice cake, in 2 cm cubes
  • 200 gfirm tofu, cubed and pan-fried until golden
  • 200 gbeansprouts, blanched briefly
  • for sauce120 g roasted peanuts, 3 cloves raw garlic, 2 bird's-eye chillies, 1 Tbsp palm sugar, 3 Tbsp kecap manis, 1 Tbsp tamarind water, salt
  • to serveprawn crackers, fried shallots, sliced scallion, lime

Method — cara

  1. In a mortar, pound peanuts with the raw garlic, chillies, palm sugar and a pinch of salt. The raw garlic is essential — do not substitute.
  2. Loosen with kecap manis, tamarind, and 4–6 Tbsp hot water until you have a thick, spoonable sauce.
  3. Arrange vermicelli, rice cubes, fried tofu and beansprouts in a wide bowl.
  4. Pour the sauce generously over the top. Garnish with shattered prawn crackers, fried shallots, scallion and a wedge of lime.
  5. Toss at the table just before eating, so the textures stay distinct.
Chef's note

Ketoprak lives or dies by its garlic. Use it raw, use it bold — a half-clove will give you a polite sauce; the polite sauce is the wrong sauce.

№012
East Java  /  Surabaya

Tahu Telur

A tofu omelette deep-fried, smashed, drowned in peanut soy.

Serves
2
Time
25 min
Difficulty
Easy
Region
Surabaya

Tahu telur begins with a violence and ends in a sauce. Cubed firm tofu and beaten egg are dropped together into a small pan of very hot oil; they bind into a single golden disc, laced and ragged at the edges. The cook fishes the disc out, breaks it crudely into pieces with the back of his spatula, lays the pieces over rice vermicelli and beansprouts, then pours over a thick brown sauce of peanut, kecap manis, garlic and chilli.

It is the comfort food of Surabaya — fast, cheap, deeply savoury, with the textural delight of crisp ragged edges sopping up a sweet, salty, slightly bitter sauce.

An omelette that has been broken on purpose is a kind of honesty about how omelettes get eaten anyway.

Ingredients — bahan

  • 300 gfirm tofu, in 1.5 cm cubes
  • 4eggs, beaten
  • 2 stalksscallion, sliced
  • ½ tspsalt, pinch of pepper
  • for servingrice vermicelli, beansprouts, prawn crackers, fried shallots, sliced lettuce
  • for sauce100 g roasted peanuts, 3 cloves garlic, 2 chillies, 4 Tbsp kecap manis, 1 Tbsp palm sugar, 1 tsp tamarind, 100 ml hot water

Method — cara

  1. Pound or blitz the sauce ingredients to a thick brown sauce, the consistency of pancake syrup. Set aside.
  2. Toss the tofu cubes with the beaten egg, scallion, salt and pepper. The egg should coat each cube generously.
  3. Heat 2 cm of oil in a small wok or saucepan to 180 °C.
  4. Pour the tofu-egg mixture in all at once. It will puff and brown at the edges. Do not stir.
  5. After 90 seconds, flip the disc — use a wide slotted spoon — and fry the other side, 60 seconds more.
  6. Lift onto paper. Break crudely into 4–6 pieces.
  7. To plate: a base of vermicelli, beansprouts, lettuce. Lay the broken omelette pieces over. Pour the sauce until it pools at the edges. Crown with fried shallots and prawn crackers.
Chef's note

The oil must be very hot, or the egg will be flat and greasy. Drop a single bead of batter in first — it should sizzle and bob immediately.

№013
West Java  /  Sundanese

Cilok

Tapioca balls, poked with a stick, sauced with peanut.

Serves
4 as snack
Time
30 min
Difficulty
Easy
Region
Sunda

The name cilok contracts the Sundanese phrase aci dicolok — "starch that has been poked." That is, more or less, the entire dish: tapioca starch dumplings, kneaded with garlic and pepper, steamed or boiled until they bounce, threaded onto a bamboo stick, then dragged through a peanut sauce, with a slick of kecap manis and a sweet chilli sauce for emphasis.

It is West Java's after-school snack, sold by men on bicycles with a small steamer in the basket. It costs almost nothing, takes seconds to assemble, and disappears in about the same length of time.

Cilok asks one question of its dough: bounce, or no bounce? Wrong answer and the children will go elsewhere tomorrow.

Ingredients — bahan

  • 200 gtapioca starch
  • 80 gplain flour
  • 300 mlboiling water
  • 4 clovesgarlic, finely grated
  • 1 tspsalt, ½ tsp white pepper, ½ tsp bouillon
  • 2 stalksscallion, very finely sliced
  • for sauce120 g roasted peanuts, 2 garlic, 2 chillies, 3 Tbsp kecap manis, 1 Tbsp palm sugar, 200 ml hot water
  • to servekecap manis, sambal, bamboo skewers

Method — cara

  1. In a wide bowl, whisk plain flour with garlic, salt, pepper, bouillon and scallion. Pour over boiling water, stirring until you have a thick paste.
  2. Add tapioca starch in two stages, kneading first with a spoon then with a (lightly oiled) hand, until you have a smooth, slightly tacky dough.
  3. Shape into balls the size of small walnuts. Oil your palms if the dough sticks.
  4. Bring a wide pot of water to a gentle boil. Drop the cilok in. They will sink, then rise. Cook 4 minutes after they rise. Lift out.
  5. For the sauce: pound peanuts, garlic and chillies to a paste. Loosen with kecap manis, palm sugar and hot water in a small pan; simmer 5 minutes.
  6. Thread 4–5 cilok onto each skewer. Spoon peanut sauce generously over. Drizzle extra kecap manis. Eat warm.
Chef's note

The dough must be hot when you knead it — cold tapioca becomes powdery, hot tapioca becomes elastic. This is the entire physics of cilok.

№014
West Sumatra  /  Minangkabau

Rendang

The long, dark cook — beef, coconut, and four hours of patience.

Serves
6
Time
3 h 30 min
Difficulty
Project
Region
Minangkabau

Rendang is what coconut milk becomes when no one in the kitchen is in a hurry. Chunks of beef are dropped into a wide pan of coconut milk that has been pounded with a great spice paste — shallots, garlic, chillies, lemongrass, galangal, ginger, turmeric, candlenut, kaffir lime leaf, salam, turmeric leaf. The pan simmers. Hours pass.

The coconut milk first reduces to a curry (gulai); then, as more water evaporates and the oils begin to split, to a darker, drier braise (kalio); and then, with continued stirring and patience, to the deep, almost-black, oil-glazed final state called rendang. The beef has not been frying — it has been slow-toasting in its own caramelised coconut. It will keep for weeks.

Rendang is a sauce that has eaten itself, leaving only its essence behind. — A Padang grandmother, anywhere in West Sumatra

Do not rush. A pressure cooker will give you a beef stew. The hours are not negotiable; they are the recipe.

Ingredients — bahan

  • 1.2 kgbeef chuck or shin, in 4 cm cubes
  • 1.5 Lthick coconut milk (or 800 ml + 700 ml water)
  • 2kaffir lime leaves, 4 salam leaves
  • 2turmeric leaves, knotted (if available)
  • 2 stalkslemongrass, bruised
  • for the paste15 shallots, 8 cloves garlic, 15 red long chillies (deseeded for less heat), 6 bird's-eye chillies, 1 thumb ginger, 2 thumbs galangal, 1 thumb fresh turmeric, 5 candlenuts, 1 Tbsp coriander seed, 1 tsp shrimp paste
  • to tastepalm sugar, salt, tamarind water

Method — cara

  1. Pound or blend all paste ingredients to a smooth purée.
  2. In a wide, heavy pan, combine the paste with coconut milk and aromatics (lemongrass, kaffir lime, salam, turmeric leaf). Bring to a gentle simmer, stirring.
  3. Add the beef. Simmer uncovered, stirring every 10 minutes. After about 60 minutes the sauce will thicken into a yellow gravy — this is the gulai stage. You could eat it here. Do not.
  4. Continue cooking. After 90 minutes more, the sauce will have darkened to a thick caramel-brown coating each piece of beef — this is kalio. You could eat it here. Some do.
  5. Continue cooking, stirring more frequently now to prevent catching. The oil will release and the mass will turn almost black. About 60 minutes more.
  6. At the very end — when only a few tablespoons of dark oil remain — season with palm sugar, salt and a small splash of tamarind. Stir, taste, adjust.
  7. Lift onto a plate. Serve with rice, two days from now, when the flavour has deepened further.
Chef's note

Use a heavy pan with a wide surface area — evaporation is the engine of rendang. A narrow stockpot will keep the sauce wet, and you will only ever reach kalio.

№015
West Java  /  Bandung

Seblak

After-school chaos — rehydrated crackers in a hot, aromatic mess.

Serves
2
Time
25 min
Difficulty
Easy
Region
Bandung

Seblak is what happens when you take dried prawn crackers — the puffy, brittle ones you'd eat alongside satay — and refuse to let them be crisp. You boil them. You shock them with chilli, garlic and kencur (an aromatic ginger relative whose scent is sharp, almost camphor-bright). You crack in an egg. You throw in whatever else is around: macaroni, instant noodles, meatballs, fishcake, sausage, chicken feet if the day calls for them. The crackers go translucent and chewy. The whole thing arrives in a wide bowl looking like a controlled accident.

It is a millennial dish, born in Bandung in the 2000s, sold by university canteens and TikTok stalls. There is no canonical version. The constant is kencur — without it, you have a noodle soup; with it, you have seblak.

Seblak smells, properly made, like a kitchen mid-argument.

Ingredients — bahan

  • 100 guncooked prawn crackers (kerupuk)
  • 80 gmacaroni or instant noodles
  • 2eggs
  • 100 gfishcake, sliced meatballs, sausage — any or all
  • 4–6bird's-eye chillies, to taste
  • 4 clovesgarlic
  • 3candlenuts
  • 1 thumbkencur (essential) — or 1 tsp ground kencur
  • 2 stalksscallion, sliced
  • 1 Tbspkecap manis, salt, bouillon, splash of fish sauce
  • to servefried shallots, sliced lime, more chilli

Method — cara

  1. Soak the prawn crackers in hot water for 10 minutes until they soften and bend without breaking. Drain.
  2. Boil the macaroni separately to al dente; drain.
  3. Pound chillies, garlic, candlenuts and kencur to a fragrant paste — kencur should dominate the smell.
  4. Heat 2 Tbsp oil in a wok over high heat. Fry the paste until oil splits and it smells almost too strong, 2 minutes.
  5. Crack in the eggs and scramble loosely into the paste.
  6. Add the fishcake, meatballs, sausage. Toss 30 seconds.
  7. Add the softened crackers and macaroni with 300 ml water. Simmer 3 minutes — the crackers will turn translucent and chewy.
  8. Season with kecap manis, salt, bouillon, fish sauce. Toss in scallion. Plate, top with fried shallots, lime and extra chilli for those who don't know what they're in for.
Chef's note

Kencur is non-negotiable. Substituting ginger or galangal gives you a different (perfectly fine) dish that is not seblak.
